Government Freestyler

Six years of building one of the world’s most ambitious digital states from the inside left me with a kind of knowledge that doesn’t fit into conference talks or policy papers. At some point I realized the only honest container for it was a book.

The book is about Ukraine’s digital state — how we built it under the leadership of Mykhailo Fedorov, what it looks like to transform a government from the inside while the country is simultaneously running a pandemic response and then, without warning, a full-scale war, and what we learned that I believe no government in the world should have to learn the hard way.

But underneath all of that, it is about one thing: what it means to be a Government Freestyler. I think I found a name for a kind of person who has always existed inside the machinery of government — but never had a category.

A Government Freestyler is not a rebel. Not someone who ignores the rules or burns the system down. They are someone who refuses to accept the rules as the outer boundary of what is possible — who doesn’t wait for a mandate before taking responsibility, who doesn’t ask is this allowed, but instead what is blocking this, and what would it take to remove it.

Three things set a Freestyler apart from everyone else inside the machine.

They live simultaneously in two worlds — de jure, where titles and procedures define what exists, and de facto, where the work either gets done or it doesn’t. They have learned to speak the language of both without letting the first one kill the second.

They rewrite rules rather than circumvent them, because they understand that bureaucracy is essentially code — and code can be changed, if you know where to look and have the patience and political will to actually change it. (Thirty government regulations rewritten in the first months of full-scale war is not an exception to this. It is the proof of it.)

They stay when the moment is there, even when staying is hard, because they understand that the moment will not wait — and neither will the people who depend on what gets built inside it.

There is also a cost to all of this. The system does not love those who rewrite it, even when it accepts the results. The personal price of operating with that kind of freedom inside a structure that was not designed for it is something I write about honestly in the book — because it matters, and because almost no one talks about it.

If you work in government, advise governments, fund reform efforts, or have simply wondered why so many genuinely good ideas manage to die so completely inside the institutions that claim to want them — this book is for you.

Personal constitution

Philosophy

What I can do

I see what needs to be built. I lead the people who can build it. And I deliver at the scale of nations and the speed of startups. I have designed national-scale digital systems, launched ecosystems from zero and turned ambitious vision into working infrastructure. I move between worlds that were not designed to talk to each other: governments and founders, ministers and engineers, institutions and ecosystems and I make them fall in love with innovations and tech and produce something new together. I work where no playbook exists and I build the rules as the work demands. I call this Government Freestyler thinking.

What I believe

I believe institutions can be transformed. Ukraine taught me that. I believe technology and innovation are the most powerful tools we have to redesign how societies work, learn and govern and that those who learn to wield them responsibly will shape the next century. I believe speed and trust are not opposites. I believe the future will not be defined by institutions or startups alone, but by unlikely coalitions willing to do what neither can do alone. I believe action is the only credible language of belief.

My vision

A world where governments and businesses are co-creators of the future. Where technology and innovation expand human agency, not replace it. Where AI is the foundation of how societies learn, govern and raise their children. Where the systems we build today outlast us and serve generations we will never meet.

I build for that world. I invest in people building for that world. I write, speak and work to make that world inevitable.

I believe our duty is to design systems that outlast us, not for today’s problems, but for tomorrow’s unknowns.

I am deeply Ukrainian and I operate as a citizen of the world.

I do not just build partnerships. I architect the ecosystems where governments and businesses become co-creators of the future.
